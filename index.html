Concrete benches made from recycled materials warm naturally during morning sun exposure, creating comfortable seating that doesn't feel cold against elderly skin. The atmospheric sprinkler system operates on fifteen-minute cycles that families learn to anticipate, creating natural rhythm breaks where children can play while adults coordinate care responsibilities and share thermal management strategies with other families.

Sistema de Alerta Térmica Familiar
Family Thermal Alert System

This alert system integrates Chief Heat and Drought Officer Isabél Sánchez's Metropolitan Forest project with Ana García's family thermal coordination protocols, using atmospheric data from Madrid's 75-kilometer green belt of 1.5 million trees to coordinate family and community response during extreme thermal events. The Metropolitan Forest acts as a natural cooling system and carbon filter that provides predictive data about thermal patterns, wind circulation, and air quality changes that help families plan thermal safety strategies. When temperatures exceed 42°C for more than four hours, the system automatically sends coordinated alerts using atmospheric readings from forest monitoring stations to families with elderly members, young children, or residents with thermal-sensitive medical conditions. The Vásquez extended family uses this system to coordinate care for 84-year-old grandfather Antonio, who requires thermal monitoring during heat emergencies: daughter Carmen receives alerts about optimal thermal transport times based on forest wind patterns and cooling corridor availability, son-in-law David coordinates with community cooling centers while accessing real-time data about forest-adjacent cooling zones where natural temperature drops provide relief, and teenage grandson Alex helps monitor Antonio's thermal comfort using simple check-in protocols that include forest-accessible walking routes during moderate heat periods. The system integrates community thermal infrastructure with the Metropolitan Forest's natural cooling capacity, providing real-time information about cooling station availability, thermal transition space optimal timing, and forest-adjacent areas where natural shade and improved air quality from the tree canopy create comfortable refuge zones. Families develop thermal emergency plans that include both built infrastructure and natural forest resources, with neighbors coordinating to check on vulnerable residents while sharing access to forest cooling corridors and biodiversity-rich areas where birds, insects, and wildlife create additional environmental comfort during extended heat periods.
